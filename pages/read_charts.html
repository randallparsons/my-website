What is a "Chord Chart", and how does it work?


Anatomy of a Chord Chart

What it all means...

This chart represents the fret board as if you were holding it right in front of your face.  The darker line on the top of this diagram represents what we call the "nut". This is similar to the bridge at the other end of the guitar (where the soundhole is), but the nut is the end of the neck farthest from the body of the guitar which connects to what is called the "head" (which is where the tuning machines are located.)
      The horizontal lines represent the six guitar strings from the lowest note string on the left to the highest pitch string to the right.  The frets are the horizontal lines, and are numbered starting from the nut to fret #1, and this chart goes up to fret #5.  Depressing a string at a any fret will produce a note that is a semi-tone higher than it would at the nearest fret that is closer to the nut, and likewise, would create a note that is a semi-tone lower than the nearest fret one the other side closer to the bridge.  You can see once you know your way around a little just how simple and direct a representation this is.  You don't really need to know anything about music to be able to look at a chart such as this and figure out how to place your fingers to make a chord!

Open Strings, "The Grid", and the First Position:

The placement of the fingers may seem alien at first.  You ideally want clean direct contact between your finger tip and the fret board on top of the string.  It is important to know that you are not putting your finger on top of the fret itself!  You are ideally placing your fingertip and depressing the string against the fret board just behind the fret.  Too close to the fret and you will deaden the string, but too far back and you will get string-buzz.  Another important point is that your leverage against the string will be best if you can achieve a contact point that is nearly perpendicular to the fret board.

Open Chords and Positions Up & Down the Neck:

You do not need to do the pinch of death when holding a pick.  You want just enough pressure to keep it in your grasp, but not so tight that you can't glide across the strings.  Think of the pick as a surfboard and the strings as the waves.  You just want a clean smooth glide.  You are not trying to saw the strings in half!

Full / Partial Bar Chords and Chord Fragments

The ideal hand placement is with the outer heal of your hand (up through the line of the outside of your pinky) resting lightly against the outside of the bridge of the guitar near the soundhole and pick-guard.  The outside of the wrist can be kept bouncing against the bridge as you strum, just try not to interfere too much with the strings (unless of course you are working on more percussive strumming!)
